Ensuring a principled approach to comparative RWE

            With increasing opportunity for RWE to contribute to regulatory and other healthcare
            decision making, it is important to:

               ĘEnsure transparency and documentation of study procedures and decisions
               ĘEnsure sufficient precision and study validity
               ĘHave a thorough assessment of confounding by indication and other systematic biases

            Amgen's Center for Observational Research is implementing "clean room" and
            "staging" frameworks1

               ĘControlled and tracked access to study data and results by role
               ĘPre-specified "checkpoints" in the study timeline ensure analytic decisions are made prior to seeing results
               ĘA similar framework is being implemented for RCT Duplicate, within the Sentinel Innovation Center, and among

                 other researchers2, 3

Staging and Clean Room Framework Ensure Trust and
Minimize Bias in RW Comparative Studies

                  Staging and clean room frameworks are implemented to provide transparency and
                  documentation of a process minimizing biases in RW comparative studies

                  A comparative effectiveness study of evolocumab is one of several in which we
                  have applied these frameworks, using Target Trial Emulation and robust adjustment
                  for potential confounding.

                  Our findings provide complementary knowledge to trial results, extending the
                  evidence for effectiveness of evolocumab to a large real-world cohort of patients
                  with ASCVD

Background

HTA bodies have increasingly issued guidance on RWE, aiming to build trust
through strengthening causal inference in non-randomised studies.

`Next generation' causal analytics, characterised by
Ę Explicit causal estimands,
Ę data-driven modelling, and
Ę modern inference strategies,
places a greater focus on the statistical estimation step:

         "How is the target causal quantity estimated from observed data?"

and use of coherent causal frameworks to structure design, identi cation,
estimation, diagnostic testing, and interpretation.

if
Objectives

To assess the extent to which current international HTA and regulatory RWE
guidance support:

(i) the estimation step as a distinct and consequential component of causal
analyses,

(ii) the framing of recommendations within articulated causal frameworks.

Also to consider whether more recent developments in estimation, namely,
doubly robust methods and machine learning are re ected in existing guidance.

A Focused Review of HTA guidance on RWE and causal inference

We considered recommendations around:

1. Articulation of causal questions and estimands
2. Clear separation of identi cation assumptions from estimation choices
3. Selection of estimation approaches
4. Expectations for diagnostics and sensitivity analyses
5. Whether and how causal frameworks (e.g., target trial emulation, causal

    roadmap) are used or referenced to organise guidance

1. Estimands

Estimands, as described in ICH E9(R1) guidance, were acknowledged but rarely
presented explicitly.

Most documents anchored the causal question using PICO/PICOTS framing
(population, intervention, comparator, outcomes, timing, setting)
without recommending to systematically and fully specify estimand attributes

Some are slightly more speci c, e.g.:
MHRA guideline requires explicit estimand alignment between external control
arms and the trial using the ICH E9(R1) attributes

if
2. Separation of causal identi ability and statistical estimation

Most frameworks: A design- rst posture, emphasising protocol or SAP pre-
speci cation, identifying bias or confounding threats, and overall transparency
without clearly structuring recommendations into:
1. The identi cation assumptions required for causal interpretation,
2. The statistical inference choices that follow.
i.e, without providing a formal identi cationŁestimation work ow.

Examples of aspects mentioned:
Ę Exchangeability and positivity as prerequisites for nonrandomised comparisons
Ę Causal models, e.g. directed acyclic graphs (DAGs)) for identi ability

   considerations [NICE 2022, CADTH 2023, IQWIG 2020].
Ę Speci c methods focus, e.g., NICE DSU TSD 17.

lf if if if if if i i f f
3. Estimator selection

More detailed estimator guidance from technical and synthesis-focused
documents, e.g., estimator families (regression, propensity-score methods, IV,
quasi-experimental approaches) and their assumptions/practical implementations
in NICE DSU TSD 17. This document is now over a decade old.

HAS (2021) and IQWiG (A19-43; General Methods) primarily offered a list of
potential causal estimators, though without clear assistance for selection
between them [HAS 2021, IQWIG 2020].

Advanced estimation approaches were rarely considered in depth, remained
either method-agnostic (`use appropriate methods'), or explicitly kept statistical-
method choice out of scope [PBAC 2016, CADTH 2023].
3. Estimator selection

NICE's DSU TSD 17, included a dedicated discussion of "doubly robust methods"
(combining outcome regression and weighting/propensity models)

The EU HTA CG Practical Guideline also explicitly noted doubly robust method
(e.g., augmented IPW) as an option to reduce bias after matching/weighting when
some imbalance remains [EU HTA CG 2024b].

Most other HTA guidance (e.g., NICE's RWE framework, HAS 2021, IQWiG
documents) focus on more traditional estimator families (regression, propensity
scores, MSM/IPW, IV, DiD/RDD) either mentioning the option of DR estimators,
or not explicitly mentioning them [NICE 2022, HAS 2021, IQWIG 2020]].

No identi ed guidance documents described Super Learner approaches or
ensemble nuisance estimation.

if
4. Diagnostic tests

Documents asked for "robustness checks," sensitivity analyses, and transparency
about assumptions, but did not specify:

Ę Which diagnostics should be run
Ę How to interpret them
Ę How diagnostic failures should change the analytic plan

Where diagnostic guidance is provided, e.g., NICE DSU TSD 17, EU HTA CG,
IQWiG, HAS, the focus is mostly on propensity score overlap/balance/distribution
to judge feasibility of PS methods.
5. Use of causal frameworks

Where mentioned, target trial emulation (TTE) was the most common [Hernan 2016].

Ę IQWiG explicitly recommended considering target trial emulation for comparative
   routine data studies [IQWIG 2020]

Ę ENCePP and NICE's RWE Framework go further by presenting target trial
   emulation as a more concrete organising principle [NICE 2022, ENCePP 2023].

Ę NICE DSU TSD 17 uses counterfactual (Rubin) causal model framing to distinguish
   causal from associational comparisons and to motivate adjustment strategies [Faria
   2015].

Ę EU HTA CG 2024b] describes causal assumptions such as exchangeability, similarity,
   homogeneity, and consistency that need to be met prior to use of certain analytical
   approaches.

   Bias vs Design Emulation Differences

    Expected to shift the target question for RWE study vs RCT?

          a) Start of follow up in hospital (hospital Rx data not available in claims, but may be
                available in linked data)

          b) Run-in that selects responders to one treatment arm
          c) Mixing effect of randomization and discontinuation of baseline maintenance therapy
          d) Delayed effect over long follow up
          e) Differences in population distribution coupled with effect modification
          f) Inadequate emulation of the exposure or outcome

        Few design emulation differences = None of { a, b, c, d } AND comparator and

          outcome emulation are at least moderate, with >1 classified as good

        More design emulation differences = a OR b OR c OR d OR poor comparator

          emulation OR neither comparator and outcome emulation are classified as good

Generating RWE in Practice

Ę The article by Muntner et al. established a robust framework to promote transparency and
   mitigate bias in comparative analyses of real-world data (RWD).

Ę Wang et al. provided guidance for using real-world evidence (RWE) to emulate randomized
   controlled trials (RCTs) and highlighted important challenges to successful emulation,
   including incomplete capture of confounding factors, insufficient details on
   inclusion/exclusion criteria, lack of accurate measurement for exposures and outcomes, and
   issues with missing data. Additional barriers cited by the authors were differences in patient
   populations, variations in treatment definitions, and practical difficulties in replicating trial
   protocols--such as randomization, timing, and follow-up.

Ę The recent FDA position paper on single clinical trials further expands the opportunities for
   utilizing RWE/RWD in clinical development.
Generating RWE in Practice (Cont'd)

Ę Implementation challenges remain

   Establish a comprehensive infrastructure for leveraging innovative study designs, advanced
      analytics, and use cases. AbbVie is actively developing this infrastructure to enhance capabilities
      in generating real-world evidence.

   Develop standardized approaches and checklists to assess the suitability and regulatory-grade
      quality of real-world data (RWD).

   Create and maintain a global RWD catalogue capturing available data sources worldwide.

   Collect and document use cases in study design, advanced analytics, best practices, and lessons
      learned to foster continued improvement.

   Emphasize education as a critical driver for successful implementation and sustainability.
